Stage 1 – Sunday, July 19 – La Sage, Col du Tsaté, Cabane de Moiry

Map

The opening stage of our Haute Route – and straight into the discipline that defines this trek: crossing from one valley into the next over a high pass. The classic stage starts down in Les Hauderes (the Komoot plan says 13.2 km, 1,923 m of ascent, 7:10 h) – we joined in the mountain village of La Sage instead, saving ourselves the first 400 vertical metres on tarmac and meadow paths. That still leaves a good 11 km with around 1,650 m up and 500 m down: through larch forest to the alpine huts of the Tsaté, over the 2,868 m pass and along the glacier forefield to the Cabane de Moiry on the far side.

Tip: Starting in La Sage instead of Les Hauderes is perfectly legitimate – the PostBus stops right in the village (“La Sage, village”). You will want those saved vertical metres for the final pull up to the hut in the afternoon.

Start in La Sage (1,667 m): The little village clings to the sunny flank of the Val d’Hérens, high above Les Hauderes – all around are the sun-blackened timber houses and granaries (mazots) typical of the Valais, perched on round stone “mushroom” discs to keep the mice away from the grain. Beyond the last houses the trail starts climbing immediately, first across meadows, then through open larch forest.

Final act before the climb: travel shoes off, hiking boots on – at the edge of the path between the first granaries of La Sage.

Alpage du Tsaté (approx. 2,170 m): Above the forest the terrain opens up and you suddenly step into another century: the du Tsaté alpage is a cluster of a dozen low stone huts roofed with heavy stone slabs – cattle are still summered up here today. A large Swiss flag hangs on one of the huts, and behind it the view runs down the whole Val d’Hérens to the peaks on the far side.

The du Tsaté huts with their stone-slab roofs – and the obligatory Swiss flag. From here it is still a good 700 vertical metres to the pass.

Col du Tsaté (2,868 m): The crossing from the Val d’Hérens into the Val de Moiry, a side valley of the Val d’Anniviers. The last half hour grinds through scree and rubble, but the reward at the top is all the greater: on the far side an entirely new mountain world opens up – the first meltwater lakes of the glacier forefield shimmer in the green basin below, and behind them the three-thousanders around the Glacier de Moiry stack up.

Looking east from the Col du Tsaté: the basin with the forefield lakes below, our descent path winding down the scree flank.

Lac de Moiry: On the descent from the pass the reservoir appears below to the right – a turquoise so unreal you first suspect a photo wallpaper. The colour comes from finely ground rock flour (“glacial milk”) that the Glacier de Moiry washes into the lake. The 148 m arch dam at the northern end was completed in 1958; the water feeds the power stations of the Val d’Anniviers.

Lac de Moiry (2,249 m) and its dam – the turquoise is real, no filters involved.

The traverse to the hut: Instead of dropping to the lake, the trail contours high above the head of the valley towards the south. Below, the glacier forefield spreads out: the milky-turquoise Lac de Châteaupré, braided meltwater streams, freshly polished rock slabs – and above it all the Glacier de Moiry pushes its shattered icefall into the valley. The final stretch follows the bare crest of the moraine – ice to the left, scree to the right, the hut straight ahead on its rock bar.

On the high trail: Lac de Châteaupré and the braided meltwater channels below, the Glacier de Moiry to the right.

On the moraine crest just before the hut – behind us the meltwater cascades out of the icefall.

Cabane de Moiry (2,825 m): The hut of the SAC Montreux section sits on a rock bar directly above the icefall of the Glacier de Moiry – you can hardly get closer to a glacier on foot. In 2010 the old stone house of 1924 received its striking extension: a dark steel structure with floor-to-ceiling panorama glazing, so you look straight onto the séracs over dinner. We make it up before the afternoon mist rolls in – time for tea, a card game and the first hut evening of the trek.

The Cabane de Moiry: the 2010 extension with its panorama glazing on the left, the icefall on the right – the terrace benches are front-row seats.

Made it – proof shot in front of the hut, stage 1 done.

Arrived: tea on the terrace, the card game already waiting – outside, the afternoon mist drifts across the glacier.

Closing time at 2,825 m: boots off, crocs on – the glacier view is part of the evening programme.

The icefall up close: From the hut’s rock bar it is only a few steps until you stand face to face with the Glacier de Moiry. Where the ice breaks over the rock step, the séracs pile up into a chaotic labyrinth of towers and crevasses, and below them the meltwater plunges in white cascades over the polished rock. The freshly exposed patches of bare rock in the middle of the ice show mercilessly how much the glacier has retreated in recent years.

Below the icefall the meltwater shoots over the rock step – its roar accompanies the whole hut evening.

Séracs in detail: where the glacier flows over the steep step, the ice tears into towers the size of houses.

Good night in the dormitory – the next stage is waiting tomorrow.

Tip: The Cabane de Moiry books out quickly in summer – reserve half board well in advance. And pack light hut shoes (or crocs): hiking boots stay downstairs in the boot room.
